Kwak observes a clear pattern from 13 years of working with brand CEOs: successful companies ask very fundamental questions — 'I want to do X, what do I need?' When those same companies hit a turning point toward failure, the CEO has already decided the method and dictates it. The moment questions stall — when leaders are too certain of the answer to listen — it's a warning signal equivalent to platform-level alerts.

The speed-of-action corollary matters equally: identifying a problem today and attempting to resolve it today is what separates successful brand leaders. Good changes expand channels, reach new customers, or enter difficult-but-aligned market niches — even if they ultimately fail. Bad changes alter the structural mechanism of customer expectations: lowering cosmetic ingredient quality, changing fabric composition in hit products, distorting the deficiency-satisfaction cycle. The test: does this change serve the customer's expectation cycle, or does it serve our convenience?
