When Stan Lee's publisher rejected Spider-Man on every dimension — wrong animal, wrong age, wrong level of personal problems — Lee found a workaround. Amazing Fantasy was being cancelled, and nobody cared what went in the last issue. So he put Spider-Man on the cover of a dying magazine. It became the highest-selling issue, and the publisher came running back claiming they both loved the character.

This is a reusable pattern for founders and creators: when gatekeepers reject your idea, find a low-stakes channel where nobody is paying attention and test it there. The last issue of a cancelled magazine, a side project nobody monitors, an audience nobody is fighting over. If the idea has genuine merit, the results speak louder than any gatekeeper's objection. The key is not arguing with the gatekeeper but finding an alternative path to market validation.
