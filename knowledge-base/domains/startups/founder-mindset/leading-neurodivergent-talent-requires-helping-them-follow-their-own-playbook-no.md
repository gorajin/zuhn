Karp describes his leadership philosophy as fundamentally artistic: each Palantir product, including the military systems deployed across the Middle East, was built by 'the one person in the world that could have done it.' These are people with outlier capabilities that function similarly to neurodivergence — they decompose problems in ways that normally-smart people simply cannot.

The management challenge is that at this level of aptitude, you can't just say 'do this, idiot' — they'll respond 'you're the idiot.' Instead, Karp helps them follow their own playbook, not his. He subtly inserts elements they might miss — 'anti-dyslexifying their playbook' — while keeping everything framed around their unique strengths. The result: ex-Palantirians don't build copies of Palantir. They build entirely different companies because they learned to follow their own playbook, not Karp's. This is why Palantir alumni play a disproportionate role in defense tech — they were taught to be uniquely themselves, not to conform.
